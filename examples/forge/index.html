The machine

Rigidity is the whole product.

A mill is only as accurate as it is stiff. Forge starts from a 28 kg cast-iron base with linear rails on every axis, then drives them with closed-loop servos that catch and correct skipped steps before they ruin a part. The result is a desktop machine that behaves like a small VMC — not a router with a Dremel taped on.

  • Cast-iron core, not extruded box. Mass kills chatter the cheap way.
  • Closed-loop servos on X/Y/Z. It knows when it loses a step. It doesn't.
  • Sealed enclosure with chip tray. Coolant stays in; the noise stays down.

Until now, a maker had two bad options.

Both cost you something you didn't want to spend.

A hobby router

Cheap, but…

An aluminium-extrusion frame and open belts. Fine for plywood and signs.

  • Flexes and chatters the moment it touches metal
  • Open frame — chips and coolant go everywhere
  • Open steppers skip steps and scrap the part silently
  • Screams — you wear ear protection in your own room

A real VMC

Capable, but…

A used Tormach or Haas mini. It will absolutely cut metal.

  • Weighs a third of a tonne — needs a shop, not a desk
  • $12k–40k before tooling and an enclosure
  • Wants 240 V single-phase you may not have
  • Loud enough that neighbours notice

Forge sits in the gap

Enough cast iron and closed-loop control to cut 6061 to tolerance, in a sealed box quiet enough to run on a desk, on a normal wall outlet — for the price of a good 3D printer, not a mill.

It refuses to ruin the part

Open-loop steppers lose steps under load and you only find out when the part measures wrong. Forge's servos read their own position 1,000× a second and correct before the cut drifts — so the last pass matches the first.

A note from the bench

We built the machine we couldn't buy.

We spent three years cutting aluminium on machines that were either too flimsy to hold tolerance or too big and loud to live where we worked. Every weekend was a compromise: scrap a part, or tear down a hobby router and rebuild it stiffer.

Forge is the result. Cast iron where it matters, closed-loop where it counts, sealed and quiet so it earns a place on the bench instead of exile to a shed. It's the first one we've made that we actually reach for first.

— Mara & Tomas, Forge. Hand-assembled in Lisbon.
