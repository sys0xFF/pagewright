- **niche:** productivity
- **mood:** clean-light
- **style:** minimal, gradient, mono-type
- **palette:** bg `#FFFFFF` · ink `#1A1A1A` · accent `#F06A6A` — hot-pink-to-magenta gradient on the key hero word ('human-agent'), the coral logo mark, and the announcement bar tint
- **type:** display *TWK Lausanne / geometric grotesque sans* · body *TWK Lausanne (same family, lighter weights)* — Single warm humanist-grotesque family across the whole page; oversized airy display weight reads confident and editorial rather than corporate
- **sections:** announcement-bar › hero › logos › feature-ai-teammates › feature-team-scale › feature-productivity › how-it-works › testimonials › feature-platform-scale › cta › footer
- **signature:** The hero headline mixes inks mid-sentence: black on the framing words ("The OS for ... teams") but only the conceptual phrase "human-agent" gets a pink-to-magenta gradient — using color as a semantic highlighter on a single noun-phrase instead of styling the whole headline.
- **imagery:** Near-imageless above the fold: a faint dot-grid texture fills the vast white canvas instead of a product screenshot or illustration. Trust is carried by monochrome enterprise logos (Amazon, Accenture, J&J, Dell, Merck) and a bold "85% of Fortune 100" stat rather than hero visuals.
- **copy:** Category-defining declarative voice — claims a new product class. Hero: "The OS for human-agent teams" with subhead "Supercharge your teams to get things done".

**Takeaways (steal as ideas, don't copy):**
- Use gradient color as a semantic highlighter on ONE noun in the headline, leaving the rest in plain ink — it directs the eye to your positioning, not just decoration.
- Trade the hero screenshot for radical white space + a faint dot-grid; let an oversized 3-line headline and a single stat ('85% of Fortune 100') do the selling.
- Pair a pure-black primary pill button with a ghost/outlined secondary right beside it — high-contrast monochrome CTAs against a colorful accent word.
- Frame your product as an 'OS for [new category]' to claim a higher altitude than feature-list competitors.
