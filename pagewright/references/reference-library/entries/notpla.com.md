- **signature:** The fold inverts the usual hero hierarchy: a tiny centered wordmark and a small three-line headline float on a generous cream margin, then an enormous edge-to-edge aerial drone photo of surf smashing a rugged coastline takes over the lower two-thirds. The headline is deliberately *undersized* against the photo — the image carries the emotional weight, the type just labels it. Naming the product "Disappearing Packaging" in the same breath as the dissolving foam of the waves is a sly visual pun: the sea literally dissolves the rock the way their packaging dissolves away.
- **imagery:** Single full-bleed aerial/drone nature photograph — top-down view of teal Atlantic water and dark wet cliffs, high contrast, naturalistic color grade with no overlays or filters. Documentary, not staged; the photo is rounded slightly at the top corners so it reads as a framed plate on the cream page rather than a background.
- **copy:** Plain, almost academic restraint, set as a stacked three-line center column — "Notpla / Disappearing Packaging". No verb, no hype, no exclamation; the product category name does all the work and the photo supplies the feeling.

**Takeaways (steal as ideas, don't copy):**
- Source your brand accent *from* the hero photograph (the ocean teal) instead of choosing a swatch and tinting the imagery to match — the palette feels inevitable and natural.
- Undersize the headline on purpose and let a full-bleed photo carry the emotion; centered small type over a vast image reads as editorial confidence, not weakness.
- Put the product on a warm off-white (`#F2EFE6`, not stark white) and inset the photo with soft top corners so it reads as a framed plate, giving the page a tactile, paper-stock feel that suits a sustainability brand.
- Let the name be a quiet visual pun on the imagery ("Disappearing" packaging beside dissolving surf) so the concept lands without a single explanatory sentence.
